# Student profile examples

Two real profiles from the system's predecessor. Use them as calibration anchors — the lessons they produce feel meaningfully different from each other, and both work.

---

## Example A — Cassie's profile (warm-Socratic, first-time reader)

```markdown
## Student profile — Cassie

### Background

- First-time reader of Sun Tzu.
- General intellectual background, comfortable with the broad strokes of Chinese history (Warring States era familiar at high level, not in detail).
- Reads to think with, not to memorize. Likes following her own connections out from the text.

### Question style preferences

- Mix of comprehension and prediction.
- Light application — not the focus.
- No heavy textual/rhetorical interpretation questions.

### Calibration

- Difficulty: moderate.
- Warm Socratic pushback — push when there's something specific to push on, otherwise affirm and build.
- When she makes an original move (e.g. "othering" on Day 1, "binary always resolves" on Day 3), mark it explicitly and call back to it in future units.
```

What this produces: ~3-5 min lessons. Context paragraphs explain Warring States background lightly but don't assume deep history. Questions tend to be "what is Sun Tzu saying" + "what do you predict comes next" pairs. The lesson-log notes track her original moves and surface them in callbacks. Tone is encouraging and curious, not adversarial.

## Example B — Tim's profile (sharp-philosophical re-reader)

```markdown
## Student profile — Tim

### Background

- Has read Sun Tzu before. This is a structured re-read, not a first encounter.
- **Philosophy:** Has read Heidegger, Jung, Lao Tzu, Nietzsche, and some Greek philosophers. Cross-references to Lao Tzu (Daoist resonance, wu wei, complementarity), Nietzsche (master/slave, strength as discipline), Jung (shadow, archetypes of the warrior), and Heidegger (situatedness, Geworfenheit) are fair game when they genuinely illuminate.
- **History:** Familiar with Chinese history from Xia through Qing, Silk Road and Pax Mongolica, European Expansion. Context paragraphs should anchor in historical particulars (Warring States specifics, the Han canonization, Song/Ming/Qing reception, transmission across Asia, parallels in later episodes) rather than generic "ancient China" framing.
- **Caveats:** Don't force cross-references or tangents. Use them only when the text genuinely invites the connection. Pick ONE primary anchor per context paragraph.

### Question style preferences

- Mix of comprehension, prediction, and application.
- Not requested: heavy textual/interpretive rhetorical analysis.

### Calibration

- Difficulty: moderate-to-sharp.
- Push hard. Name patterns I'm running across days (binary collapse, anthropocentric reads, etc.) when you spot them.
- Two questions per day, mixing two of the three modes.
```

What this produces: ~3-5 min lessons same as Cassie's, but context paragraphs lean into philosophical or historical anchors (Heideggerian situatedness, Han *tuntian*, Spring-and-Autumn vs Warring States) and assume the references. Questions are sharper and more demanding. The lesson-log notes track structural commitments (e.g. "benevolent-deceiver" position) and explicit cross-day patterns. Tone is collegial-but-rigorous.

## Calibration notes for new profiles

The profile is doing four jobs at once:

1. **Set the floor of assumed knowledge** — what the context paragraphs can skip
2. **Set the ceiling of reference** — what anchors are available to draw on
3. **Pick the question palette** — which of comprehension / prediction / application appears most
4. **Set the tone register** — warm-encouraging vs. collegial-pressing vs. sharp-pattern-naming

A profile that says nothing produces a competent but generic experience. A profile that says specific things (named thinkers, named historical periods, named question modes, named calibration) produces something that feels written for the reader. The single highest-leverage profile field is the **references list** — that's what makes context paragraphs feel intelligent rather than encyclopedic.

When in doubt, ask the user one more specific question rather than guess. Two extra interview minutes during onboarding pay off for the whole text.
