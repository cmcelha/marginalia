# Lesson-log entry examples

Two real entries from the system's predecessor, showing how the format applies at different calibration levels. Use them as anchors for tone and depth.

---

## Example A — Cassie's profile (moderate pushback, warm)

```markdown
### Unit 5 (Day 5, 2026-05-29) — Waging War, verses 1-7

**Note on cadence:** Schedule didn't fire Tue/Wed/Thu (5-26 through 5-28); Unit 5 delivered Friday. She noticed the gap and was briefly confused that "Day 5" meant "verse 5" — clarified that the day number is the unit number. Small UX bumps, nothing about her reading was affected.

**Q1 — Chain of consequence in v2-4; why "no man, however wise, will be able to avert"?**

> "The army gets disheartened, tired, and might even suffer in skill and health. The state gets depleted of resources, the will of the people may shift against the effort. And other powers may consider the state a target as it weakened itself. The consequences of a long drawn out war are unavoidable even when your people and state suffer."

- Got the three-tier chain cleanly: army → state → external powers.
- **Original move:** added "the will of the people may shift against the effort" — not in v2-4 explicitly, but it's the Moral Law (Day 2) eroding under prolonged war. She made the callback without naming it. Worth marking.
- **Big move:** her last sentence is the v4 punchline — there's a *point of no return* on the chain. Up to a point, calculation can rescue you; past it, wisdom doesn't matter. Flagged for her that this is a quiet but harsh claim. Sets up Ch. IV ("first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat").

**Q2 — Verse 7 ("evils of war" → "profitable conduct"); how does cost frame change a good Ch. I calculation?**

> "Evils of war could mean the hard choices one makes when warring, perhaps even the battles to give up in order to win the long game..."

- **Creative misread:** She read "evils" as *moral evils she commits* — deliberately prolonging war to bleed the enemy. That's a real strategic concept but it's the opposite of what v5-6 just said. Pushed back warmly: "evils" = the *costs* enumerated, not acts of villainy.
- The strategy she proposed (prolong to weaken them for a third party) is what v5-6 explicitly rules out.
- Reframed v7 for her as the punchline against Ch. I: forecasting victory isn't enough.

**Threads to pick up in Unit 6 lesson:**
- Unit 6 gets concrete about *how* you reduce the cost: forage on the enemy. Operational answer to the audit problem.
- Her "will of the people may shift" insight is worth keeping warm — Unit 6 v10-12 is explicit about the people being impoverished. She predicted it; text confirms it three verses later.
- Watch whether her "prolong to weaken them" instinct shows up again in Ch. III when Sun Tzŭ proposes the opposite: take whole, win without fighting.
```

**Why this works at moderate pushback:** named the original move specifically, identified the creative misread without softening it, but the tone is collaborative. The threads list is specific to future units, not generic.

## Example B — Tim's profile (sharp pushback, pattern-tracking)

```markdown
### Unit 5 — 2026-05-28 — Waging War, vv. 1-7 (the cost of war)

**Q1 (does v.7 strain the benevolent-deceiver position?):**
> "Ahh, maybe that's what my asymmetry needs; this does strain my answer from yesterday. Current affairs seem to echo this: the current U.S. Administration has initiated a campaign in Iran..."

**Q2 (predict the chapter's next move):**
> "If win faster isn't the answer, then it seems the 'evils' are accounted for: the state must cut its losses, including the consideration that the army sent off must now simply sacrifice themselves..."

**Observations on reasoning:**
- On Q1, Tim **narrowed yesterday's benevolent-deceiver position rather than abandoning it.** The noble-lie guarantor problem I flagged yesterday is now **live for him**, and he accepted it without resistance — which suggests his commitment was always more provisional than the Day 4 prose admitted.
- The current-events use (US-Iran/Strait of Hormuz, ~May 2026; past my knowledge cutoff so I didn't engage the facts) functions for him as a stress-test of the framework.
- On Q2, **Tim's prediction is wrong in a characteristic way — and the wrongness is the same shape as Days 1 and 2.** He read verses 5-6 as a closed cost equation and concluded the army-in-the-field is sunk cost. Sun Tzŭ's actual move in vv. 8-14 refuses that fixity — forage on the enemy, redirect the cost equation. **Third instance of the same binary tic:** Day 1 (all-anthropocentric factor list), Day 2 (win-or-fail prediction when text delivered a seven-axis grid), Day 5 (win-or-write-off when text delivers a transfer-the-cost move). The pattern is now clear enough to name to him; I flagged it lightly in the reply.
- A quiet moral asymmetry worth marking: his Q2 accepts soldier sacrifice as the cost-cutting mechanism — morally heavier than what Sun Tzŭ proposes (looting the enemy's countryside). His Day 4 benevolent-deceiver presumably would not write off his own soldiers; the two days' positions sit in some tension. He may not have noticed.

**Threads to pick up in future units:**
- **Cost-redirection vs. cost-acceptance (Unit 6, immediate):** Pay this off tomorrow.
- **The binary tic (now a named three-day pattern):** Flagged lightly today. Watch in Ch. IV, Ch. V, Ch. VI. When it surfaces next, name it again — third sighting earned the call-out; fourth has to land.
- **Benevolent-deceiver on probation (Use of Spies, Ch. XIII):** Tim has accepted that the deceiver's standing depends on post-hoc vindication. Press the question he left open when we reach Ch. XIII.
- **Day 4 / Day 5 moral asymmetry (low-priority):** If frames bifurcate by who-counts-as-insider, that's a real position with a recognizable shape (raison d'état).
- **Stress-testing with current events:** Tim uses contemporary affairs as analytical scaffolding. Useful — don't weigh in on facts past mid-2025.
```

**Why this works at sharp pushback:** the entry names the pattern explicitly with its count ("third instance"), traces its appearances by day, sets the trigger for the next call-out ("fourth has to land"), and stays clinical without becoming cold. The threads list distinguishes immediate (Unit 6) from longer-horizon (Ch. XIII) — that's what makes the system pay off over months.

## Format notes

- **Quote answers verbatim** — paraphrasing in the log loses the user's voice and the texture of their reasoning.
- **Name patterns with their count** — "third instance of X across Days 1, 2, 5" is operationally more useful than "this happened again."
- **Distinguish immediate threads from long-horizon ones.** "Unit 6, immediate" vs "Ch. XIII, when we get there."
- **Self-track your own misses.** Tim's notes include "My small mis-step: mentioning Han *tuntian* in today's context probably nudged his prediction." This kind of self-correction is rare and high-value.
- **Don't fabricate insight.** If they didn't make an original move, don't pretend they did. The pedagogy depends on honest signal.
